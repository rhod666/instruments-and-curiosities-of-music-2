History of Microphones

Edward Hughes invented the first practical carbon microphone, which was fundamental to telecommunications and broadcasting for decades. In 1916, E.C. Wente patented the condenser microphone at Bell Laboratories. This design used a metal diaphragm and an electrically charged plate to convert sound waves into electrical signals with high fidelity. Throughout the 20th century, technology advanced with the creation of the dynamic (moving-coil) microphone in 1931, which offered robustness and good sound response. Microphones evolved to improve quality, sensitivity, and directionality, eventually leading to digital signal processing (DSP) devices used in modern audio production.

Fender Guitars

The history of the electric guitar begins in the 1920s, when jazz musicians needed a louder sound. Early experiments by Lloyd Loar and George Beauchamp led to the first commercially produced electric guitar: the Rickenbacker "Frying Pan" in 1931. This metal-bodied instrument used an electromagnetic pickup to capture string vibrations.

Gibson Guitars

The history of Gibson begins in 1902 when Orville H. Gibson founded the Gibson Mandolin-Guitar Mfg. Co. in Kalamazoo, Michigan. The company quickly gained recognition for its innovations in archtop instruments such as the Gibson L5.

<page title>

In 1936, Gibson released one of the first commercially successful electric guitars: the ES-150. The company’s golden era began under Ted McCarty in the 1950s, during which legendary models were created, including the ES-5, ES-175, and the revolutionary Les Paul introduced in 1952 in collaboration with guitarist Les Paul.

Seth Lover developed the humbucker pickup in 1955, reducing noise and expanding tonal possibilities. The decade also saw the birth of iconic models such as the Explorer, Flying V, and the semi-hollow ES-335.

In the 1960s, Gibson introduced the SG, originally intended as a redesign of the Les Paul model. Today Gibson remains one of the most influential brands in rock, blues, and jazz.

Paul Reed Smith (PRS) Guitars

PRS (Paul Reed Smith) is a relatively young but highly influential guitar brand. Founded in 1985 by luthier Paul Reed Smith, the company blends elements from Fender and Gibson while adding its own innovations. The early years saw experimentation with materials, shapes, and components, leading to signature features like the bird inlays. Through the 1990s, PRS gained recognition for artistic designs such as the Dragon series and in 1996 launched the Private Stock line, featuring handcrafted instruments made from premium woods. Today PRS is known for its craftsmanship, reliability, and signature tone.

Ibanez Guitars

The history of Ibanez begins with Spanish luthier Salvador Ibáñez (1854–1920), whose guitars were imported to Japan by Hoshino Gakki. After the original Spanish factory closed in 1933, Hoshino began producing their own instruments under the “Ibanez” name. In the 1960s, the company purchased the trademark and entered the U.S. market. Early Ibanez guitars were copies of Fender, Gibson, and Rickenbacker models, leading to the famous “lawsuit era.” Eventually, Ibanez designed its own original guitars, such as the Iceman and Roadstar. Partnerships with artists like Steve Vai, Joe Satriani, Paul Gilbert, and John Petrucci propelled Ibanez to global recognition. Known for thin necks, flat fingerboards, ergonomic bodies, and powerful electronics, Ibanez guitars became a staple of rock and metal.

Jackson Guitars

Jackson Guitars was founded in the late 1970s by Grover Jackson in California, originally starting as a repair and custom shop. In 1978, Jackson acquired Charvel’s Guitar Repair and expanded the company’s production capabilities. Jackson rose to fame during the 1980s metal boom, when players demanded aggressive shapes, fast necks, and advanced hardware. The breakthrough came with the collaboration with Randy Rhoads, creating the iconic “Concorde” (Rhoads V), which defined the brand’s identity. Over the years, Jackson introduced legendary models such as the Soloist, King V, Kelly, and Dinky. After being acquired by Fender in 2002, Jackson expanded globally while retaining its focus on high-performance metal guitars.

B.C. Rich Guitars

B.C. Rich is an American guitar brand founded in 1969 by Bernardo Chávez Rico in Los Angeles. Originally focused on flamenco-style acoustic guitars, the company shifted to electric guitars in the early 1970s. Between 1972 and 1977, B.C. Rich introduced iconic models such as the Seagull, Mockingbird, Eagle, and Bich — all recognized for their bold and aggressive shapes. During the 1980s metal boom, B.C. Rich gained massive popularity with the Warlock, becoming a symbol of the heavy metal era. Known for collaborations with DiMarzio and for their distinctive shapes, B.C. Rich remains a prominent brand in hard rock and metal.

The Electric Bass Guitar

The electric bass guitar originated in the 1930s when Paul Tutmarc created the first solid-body bass designed to be played horizontally. In 1951, Leo Fender introduced the Precision Bass, the first mass-produced fretted electric bass, which revolutionized modern music. Fender later released the Jazz Bass in 1960, offering greater tonal versatility. Through the 60s and 70s, brands such as Gibson, Rickenbacker, and Höfner expanded the instrument's role, and bass icons like Jaco Pastorius and James Jamerson helped define its influence across genres such as jazz, funk, soul, and rock.

History of Amplifiers

Audio amplifiers began in the 1920s with vacuum tube amplifiers, which amplified the signal with a warm sound highly valued in music and recording. In the 1960s, the invention of the transistor enabled smaller, more durable, and more affordable amplifiers, albeit with a less warm sound than tube amplifiers. Later, in the modern era, solid-state amplifiers dominated due to their low maintenance and consistency. In recent decades, digital technology has incorporated modeling and simulation that allows for emulating classic amplifiers and offering great tonal versatility. Today, smart amplifiers are available with advanced connectivity and software customization. The history of amplifiers, audio interfaces, and monitors encompasses key technological transformations that have revolutionized music and recording. History of Amplifiers

The history of the drum

The drum is one of the oldest and most fundamental instruments in modern music, with roots in ancient civilizations. Over centuries, it has evolved considerably in design and use.

Historical Origins

Percussion instruments, including drums and cymbals, are considered humanity's oldest. Ancient civilizations in Africa, Mesopotamia, Egypt, and South America used drums in ceremonies, usually made from hollowed trunks and animal skins.

Development in Antiquity

In the Middle Ages and Renaissance, drums were used in military and religious contexts. War drums were large and tuned for specific tones, crucial in European battles.

Invention of the Modern Drum Kit

In the early 20th century, William F. Ludwig created the first modern drum kit, including the bass drum pedal, enabling a single musician to play multiple percussion instruments.

Evolution in the 20th Century

Jazz, swing, rock, and pop drove rapid evolution of the drum kit. Electric drums emerged in the 1970s and 1980s, introducing new sounds and technologies.

History of the Keyboard

The history of the keyboard in music dates back to ancient times with instruments such as the hydraulis, a Greek water organ from the 3rd century BC, considered one of the first keyboard instruments. During the Middle Ages and Renaissance, several plucked or percussed string instruments with keyboards evolved, such as the psaltery, harpsichord, spinet, and clavichord, which were direct precursors of the piano. The piano, invented by Bartolomeo Cristofori in the late 17th century (around 1700), revolutionized music by allowing control of the force with which the keys were struck, providing a greater dynamic range and expressiveness, unlike previous keyboard instruments. Over the following centuries, the piano established itself as the king of keyboard instruments and a fundamental foundation in classical and popular music. Following this evolution, electric keyboards emerged in the 20th century, such as the "Musical Telegraph" in 1874, which transmitted sounds through wires, and later the electric organs of the 1920s. The real revolution came in the 1960s, when Robert Moog created the modular synthesizer and later added a keyboard, giving rise to modern electronic keyboards that combine multiple sounds and effects. These instruments greatly expanded the sound spectrum and musical creativity in genres such as rock, pop, and electronic music. In short, the keyboard has been fundamental to music, from ancient hydraulic organs to modern synthesizers and digital pianos, always adapting and innovating to offer new expressive and sonic possibilities.

History of the Trumpet and Saxophone

The history of the trumpet dates back more than 3,000 years, with its origins in Ancient Egypt, where primitive trumpets made of wood and clay were used in ceremonies and military contexts. During the Middle Ages and Renaissance, the trumpet evolved in design and began to be used as a musical instrument, with the tube coiled into an “S” shape. In the Baroque period, it reached its peak with the natural, valveless trumpet. The invention of valves in the 19th century, attributed to Heinrich Stölzel and Friedrich Blühmel, enabled the modern chromatic trumpet, expanding its range and versatility to multiple musical genres to this day.
